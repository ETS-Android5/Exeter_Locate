Thank you  for participating in the Exeter Locate project.  In this project we and the RIBA2Reality team are developing and studying a citizen science way of locating people via the local radio environment -- WiFi and Bluetooth -- measured by their phones.  It has the potential to provide accurate location information using only a fraction of the battery power consumed by GPS location. This page explains how and why Exeter Locate collects this data.

What data is collected

GPS (Global Positioning System) and radio environment information are the primary forms of data collected, but also some accelerometer and magnetometer sensor data is also collected. GPS gives the location of your phone to relatively high accuracy (a few metres or tens of metres depending on signal strength). The accelerometer detects the orientation and movement of your device, whereas the magnetometer works like a 3D compass but is also affected by the local magnetic field.

Data on the local radio environment data is also collected. We measure which WiFi access points are available and Bluetooth Low-Energy (BLE) beacons are available. We collect the unique identifier of the WiFi access-point or BLE beacon, known as the Media Access Control (MAC) address, as well as the Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI).

Data is collected at semi-regular intervals, that is at a regular interval but with some random variation.  GPS data is collected relatively infrequently (approximately once every 5 minutes), while radio environment, accelerometer and magnetometer data are measured approximately once every minute.

No personally identifying information is collected by the app. Instead, a randomly generated unique identifier is used to tag the data collected by your phone.

How the data is collected

The Exeter Locate app will not collect data when you are away from the University’s Streatham campus.  Regular location checks are made to find out whether your phone is within the campus area, shown below. When the phone is outside the area no data is recorded, although the app will continue to check your location to determine when you re-enter the campus area. All collected data is posted to our servers via a secure encrypted internet connection.

Length of time we keep your data

This data with it identifiers for not longer than a year after the project completes in June 2021. After which a subset of this data set will be further anonymised by removing all of the random unique identifiers, and released to the public for future research and/or training.

 

Keeping your information secure

 

The privacy of volunteers in this project is taken very seriously and every effort is taken to ensure that privacy is maintained. The unique random identifier used to identify data from an individual phone is generated by an industry standard pseudorandom algorithm on the volunteer’s phone, and therefore can not be associated with any particular individual without the volunteer providing this additional detail; we will never ask you for this information. The data is sent from the device to our servers via a secure encrypted internet connection .  Which are secured with industry standard techniques.

Since the app repeatedly records location information, it will also be possible to track routes taken by volunteers’ phones while on the Streatham campus, although this data remains anonymised.  It might be possible to infer an individual’s likely identity from this location data, but it is impossible to confirm this through the data itself.

 

Your information rights

 

If you would like to stop contributing data to the project at any time, the application is controlled through the start/stop button and clearly displays whether data is currently being collected.  You can, of course, delete the app from your phone.

We never record any identifiable information about the owner of the phone.  As mentioned above, data collected is tagged with a unique randomly generated identifier, but we will never ask you for this identifier.

The rights under articles 15 to 20 of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) do not apply if the University of Exeter is not in a position to identify a specific individual, unless the individual provides additional information to enable identification. Only providing the unique random identifier does not establish a definite link between that individual and the anonymised location and radio data.

This also means we are unable to provide data in response to any request to access the location and radio environment data generated by your device.

Should you have a query about how your data is handled please contact the RIBA2Reality project team at riba2reality@exeter.ac.uk
